⚛ Quantum Physics Foundations

The birth of modern physics — when classical physics broke down at atomic scales

💡 Idea of Quantization — The Beginning of Quantum Physics

Foundation▶

In the late 1800s, classical physics hit a wall. It predicted that a heated object should radiate infinite energy at high frequencies — a prediction so absurd it was called the Ultraviolet Catastrophe. Classical electromagnetism said energy could be emitted continuously, in any amount, which led to this nonsensical result.

In 1900, Max Planck made a radical proposal: energy is NOT continuous. Instead, it is emitted and absorbed in tiny, indivisible packets called quanta (singular: quantum). Each quantum carries energy proportional to its frequency:

Planck's Quantization
$$E = nh\nu \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s is Planck's constant — one of the most fundamental numbers in physics.

For a single quantum (photon): $E = h\nu = hc/\lambda$

Why this mattered: By forcing energy to come in discrete steps instead of being continuous, Planck's formula perfectly matched experimental blackbody radiation data. This single idea — that nature is "grainy" at tiny scales — is the foundation of ALL quantum mechanics.

Einstein (1905) took Planck's idea further: light itself is made of discrete particles called photons. Each photon carries exactly $E = h\nu$. This explained the photoelectric effect: $KE_{max} = h\nu - \phi$, where $\phi$ is the work function (minimum energy to free an electron from the metal).

⚠ Classical view: Energy = continuous wave. Quantum view: Energy = discrete packets (photons). This isn't just a math trick — it reflects reality at atomic scales.

☢ Compton Effect — Proof That Photons Have Momentum

★ Theory + Numerical▶

In 1923, Arthur Compton fired X-rays at free electrons (in graphite) and made a shocking discovery: the scattered X-rays had a longer wavelength than the incident ones. Classical wave theory couldn't explain this — waves don't change frequency during scattering.

Compton explained it by treating X-rays as particles (photons) colliding with electrons, just like billiard balls. The photon loses some energy to the electron → its frequency drops → wavelength increases.

Compton Shift Formula
$$\Delta\lambda = \lambda' - \lambda = \frac{h}{m_0 c}(1 - \cos\theta)$$

Key values to memorize:

Compton wavelength of electron: $\displaystyle\frac{h}{m_0 c} = \frac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{9.1\times10^{-31}\times3\times10^8} = 0.02426$ Å

At $\theta = 0°$: $\Delta\lambda = 0$ (no shift — photon goes straight)

At $\theta = 90°$: $\Delta\lambda = 0.02426$ Å

At $\theta = 180°$: $\Delta\lambda_{max} = 0.04852$ Å (backscattering — maximum shift)

Why there's an "unmodified" peak too: Some photons interact with tightly-bound electrons. In this case, the entire atom recoils (huge mass $M \gg m_e$), so the Compton shift $h/(Mc)$ is negligibly small → wavelength barely changes.

Physical significance: Compton effect proves conclusively that photons have momentum $p = h/\lambda = E/c$. Light behaves as both wave (interference, diffraction) and particle (photoelectric, Compton).

🌊 de Broglie Hypothesis — Matter Has Wave Nature

★ Numerical▶

If light (classically a wave) behaves like particles, then shouldn't particles (like electrons) behave like waves? In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed exactly this:

"Every moving particle has an associated wave — a matter wave — whose wavelength is inversely proportional to its momentum."

de Broglie Wavelength
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$

Derived forms:

Using kinetic energy $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ → $p = \sqrt{2mKE}$:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mKE}}$$

Electron accelerated through potential difference $V$: $KE = eV$

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2meV}} = \frac{12.26}{\sqrt{V}} \;\text{Å} \quad\text{(quick formula for electrons)}$$

Why we don't see wave nature in everyday life: A cricket ball ($m = 0.15$ kg, $v = 40$ m/s) has $\lambda = h/(mv) = 1.1 \times 10^{-34}$ m — billions of times smaller than a proton! Wave effects only become visible when $\lambda$ is comparable to the size of the system (atomic scales, ~Å).

🔬 Davisson-Germer Experiment — Proving Matter Waves

4-5 marks▶

Purpose: The first experimental proof that electrons (matter) exhibit wave behavior — confirming de Broglie's hypothesis.

Setup & Procedure:

  1. An electron gun produces electrons accelerated through voltage $V = 54$ V
  2. These electrons hit a nickel crystal target
  3. A movable Faraday cup detector measures scattered electron intensity at various angles
  4. A strong intensity peak was found at scattering angle $\theta = 50°$

Analysis — Two independent calculations of λ:

Method 1 (Bragg's law): The Ni crystal planes (spacing $d = 0.91$ Å) act as a diffraction grating. Glancing angle $\phi = 90° - 50°/2 = 65°$.
$2d\sin\phi = n\lambda$ → $\lambda_{exp} = 2 \times 0.91 \times \sin 65° = 1.65$ Å

Method 2 (de Broglie): $\lambda = 12.26/\sqrt{V} = 12.26/\sqrt{54} = 1.67$ Å

✦ Verification Result

$\lambda_{experiment} = 1.65$ Å  ≈  $\lambda_{de\;Broglie} = 1.67$ Å  ✓

Electrons diffract just like X-rays → wave nature of matter confirmed experimentally!

Ψ Wave Function & Born's Probability Interpretation

Foundation▶

If electrons are waves, what exactly is "waving"? Unlike water waves or sound waves, there's no physical medium oscillating. The wave function $\psi(x,t)$ is a complex-valued mathematical function that completely describes the quantum state of a particle.

$\psi$ itself has no direct physical meaning — it can even be complex! But $|\psi|^2$ tells us the probability density of finding the particle at a given position:

Born's Interpretation
$$P(x)\,dx = |\psi(x)|^2\,dx = \psi \cdot \psi^*\,dx$$

Probability of finding the particle between $x$ and $x+dx$

Total probability = 1 (particle must be somewhere):

$$\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}|\psi(x)|^2\,dx = 1 \quad \text{(Normalization condition)}$$

Properties of a physically valid $\psi$:

  • Single-valued: Only ONE value of $\psi$ at each point (physics can't be ambiguous)
  • Finite: $\psi$ can't blow up to infinity anywhere
  • Continuous: No sudden jumps in $\psi$ or its derivatives
  • Square-integrable: The total probability integral must converge → $\psi \to 0$ as $x \to \pm\infty$
💡 Deep insight: The boundary condition that $\psi = 0$ at the walls of a box forces $\lambda$ to take discrete values → discrete energies → QUANTIZATION emerges naturally from wave mechanics!

❓ Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

★ Numerical▶

Werner Heisenberg (1927) showed that certain pairs of measurements have a fundamental, built-in trade-off. The more precisely you measure one, the less precisely you can know the other:

Heisenberg Uncertainty Relations
$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p_x \geq \frac{h}{4\pi} = \frac{\hbar}{2}$$$$\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \frac{h}{4\pi} = \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

where $\hbar = h/(2\pi) = 1.054 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s

This is NOT a measurement limitation — it's a fundamental property of nature. Even with a perfect instrument, you CANNOT simultaneously know both position and momentum exactly.

Why? Think of it this way: to "see" an electron, you need to bounce a photon off it. A short-wavelength photon (high energy) gives precise position info but kicks the electron hard (large momentum uncertainty). A long-wavelength photon barely disturbs the electron but gives fuzzy position info.

Macroscopic vs microscopic: For a 1 kg ball with $\Delta x = 10^{-10}$ m (atomic!), $\Delta v = h/(4\pi m \Delta x) = 5 \times 10^{-25}$ m/s — totally negligible. For an electron with $\Delta x = 10^{-10}$ m, $\Delta v \approx 6 \times 10^{5}$ m/s — hugely significant!

⚠ Exam approach: Given $\Delta x$ → find $\Delta p_{min} = h/(4\pi\Delta x)$ → then $\Delta v = \Delta p/m$. Always use $h/(4\pi)$, not $h/(2\pi)$!

Part A — Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation ⭐
Strategy: Start with the wave equation for a free particle, take derivatives, and use the classical energy-momentum relation $E = p^2/2m + V$.

Our starting point is a plane wave representing a free particle:

Starting Point
$$\Psi(x,t) = Ae^{i(kx - \omega t)} = A\exp\!\left[\frac{i}{\hbar}(px - Et)\right]$$

using $p = \hbar k$ and $E = \hbar\omega$

Step 1Time derivative

Differentiate $\Psi$ with respect to time:

$$\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{iE}{\hbar}\Psi$$

Rearranging: $\boxed{E\Psi = i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t}}$ — the energy operator

Step 2Space derivative

Differentiate $\Psi$ twice with respect to $x$:

$$\frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial x^2} = -\frac{p^2}{\hbar^2}\Psi$$

Rearranging: $\boxed{p^2\Psi = -\hbar^2\frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial x^2}}$ — the momentum² operator

Step 3Classical energy

Total energy in classical mechanics:

$$E = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V$$

Multiply both sides by $\Psi$:

$$E\Psi = \frac{p^2}{2m}\Psi + V\Psi$$
Step 4Substitute operators

Replace $E\Psi$ with Step 1 result and $p^2\Psi$ with Step 2 result:

✦ Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation (1D)
$$\boxed{i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial x^2} + V\Psi}$$
Part B — Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation ⭐⭐
Strategy: Separate variables — assume $\Psi(x,t) = \psi(x)\cdot T(t)$ where $T(t) = e^{-iEt/\hbar}$. Substitute into TDSE.
Step 1Separation

For stationary states (constant energy): $\Psi(x,t) = \psi(x)\cdot e^{-iEt/\hbar}$

Time derivative: $i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t} = E\Psi = E\psi(x)e^{-iEt/\hbar}$

Step 2Substitute into TDSE

Since $e^{-iEt/\hbar}$ cancels from both sides:

$$E\psi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V\psi$$
Step 3Rearrange

Bring everything to one side:

✦ Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation (TISE)
$$\boxed{\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E - V)\psi = 0}$$
✦ For Free Particle ($V = 0$)
$$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}\psi = 0$$
⚠ Exam tip: Start from $\Psi = Ae^{i(kx-\omega t)}$. Show each differentiation step clearly. Box the final results. The TISE is the more frequently asked derivation.

C-1X-rays of wavelength λ = 0.2 Å are scattered by free electrons at angle θ = 90°. Find the Compton shift and the wavelength of the scattered X-rays.

📋 Given Information

Incident λ$\lambda = 0.2$ Å
Scatter angle$\theta = 90°$
Find$\Delta\lambda$ (Compton shift) and $\lambda'$ (scattered wavelength)

📐 Formula to Use

Compton shift equation:

$$\Delta\lambda = \frac{h}{m_0 c}(1 - \cos\theta) \qquad \frac{h}{m_0 c} = 0.02426 \;\text{Å}$$

1 Calculate the Compton Shift

Plug in $\theta = 90°$. Since $\cos 90° = 0$:

$\Delta\lambda = 0.02426 \times (1 - \cos 90°)$ $\Delta\lambda = 0.02426 \times (1 - 0)$ $\Delta\lambda = 0.02426$ Å
💡 Notice: at 90°, the shift equals exactly one Compton wavelength. This is because $(1 - \cos 90°) = 1$.

2 Find the Scattered Wavelength

The scattered wavelength is simply:

$\lambda' = \lambda + \Delta\lambda$ $\lambda' = 0.2 + 0.02426$ $\lambda' = 0.22426$ Å

✅ Final Answer

$\Delta\lambda = 0.02426$ Å
$\lambda' = 0.22426$ Å
💡 Physical insight: The shift is ~12% of the original wavelength. The photon lost energy to the electron → wavelength increased. At 90°, the photon and recoiling electron share the incident energy roughly equally.
C-2X-rays of wavelength λ = 1 Å undergo Compton scattering at θ = 180° (backscattering). Find the scattered wavelength and energy of the scattered photon.

📋 Given Information

Incident λ$\lambda = 1$ Å $= 10^{-10}$ m
Scatter angle$\theta = 180°$ (photon bounces straight back)

1 Compton Shift at 180°

At $\theta = 180°$, $\cos 180° = -1$, so $(1 - \cos 180°) = 2$:

$\Delta\lambda = 0.02426 \times (1 - (-1))$ $\Delta\lambda = 0.02426 \times 2$ $\Delta\lambda = 0.04852$ Å
💡 This is the MAXIMUM possible Compton shift — it happens when the photon bounces completely backward.

2 Scattered Wavelength

$\lambda' = 1 + 0.04852 = 1.04852$ Å

3 Energy of Scattered Photon

Using $E = hc/\lambda$:

$E' = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^{8}}{1.04852 \times 10^{-10}}$ $E' = \dfrac{1.988 \times 10^{-25}}{1.04852 \times 10^{-10}}$ $E' = 1.896 \times 10^{-15}$ J

Converting to keV: $E' = 1.896 \times 10^{-15} / (1.6 \times 10^{-19}) = 11,850$ eV

✅ Final Answer

$\lambda' = 1.04852$ Å
$E' = 1.896 \times 10^{-15}$ J $\approx 11.85$ keV
💡 Physical insight: The photon lost only ~5% of its wavelength even in the worst case (180°). The shift $\Delta\lambda$ is independent of $\lambda$ — it's the same whether you use X-rays or γ-rays. That's why Compton effect is noticeable only for short-wavelength X-rays (where $\Delta\lambda/\lambda$ is significant).

✅ Final Answer

$\lambda = 8.01 \times 10^{-15}$ m $\approx 8$ fm

HUP-7Compare the uncertainties in velocities of an electron and a proton confined to a 1 nm box. Given: mₑ = 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg, mₚ = 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg

📋 Given Information

Electron mass$m_e = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}$ kg
Proton mass$m_p = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg

1 Key Insight — Same Box, Same Δp

Both particles are in the same box, so they have the same $\Delta x$, hence the same $\Delta p_{min}$:

$\Delta p_{min} = \dfrac{h}{4\pi \cdot \Delta x}$ — same for both
💡 Since $\Delta v = \Delta p / m$, and $\Delta p$ is the same, the velocity uncertainty is inversely proportional to mass!

2 Find the Ratio

$\dfrac{\Delta v_e}{\Delta v_p} = \dfrac{\Delta p / m_e}{\Delta p / m_p} = \dfrac{m_p}{m_e}$ $= \dfrac{1.67 \times 10^{-27}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}$ $= 1835$

✅ Final Answer

$\Delta v_e / \Delta v_p = 1835$
The electron's velocity uncertainty is 1835 times larger than the proton's
💡 This ratio is exactly the proton-to-electron mass ratio! Lighter particles are much harder to pin down. This is why quantum effects dominate for electrons but are negligible for protons in most atomic-scale experiments.

C-4At what scattering angle will incident 100 keV X-rays leave a target with an energy of 90 keV?

📋 Given Information

Incident energy$E = 100$ keV
Scattered energy$E' = 90$ keV

📐 Formula to Use

Energy form of Compton equation:

$$\frac{1}{E'} - \frac{1}{E} = \frac{1}{m_0 c^2}(1 - \cos\theta)$$

1 Calculate LHS

$\frac{1}{E'} - \frac{1}{E} = \frac{1}{90} - \frac{1}{100} = \frac{100 - 90}{9000}$ $= \frac{10}{9000} = \frac{1}{900}$ keV$^{-1}$

2 Solve for θ

$m_0 c^2 = 511$ keV (rest energy of electron)

$(1 - \cos\theta) = m_0 c^2 \left(\frac{1}{E'} - \frac{1}{E}\right) = \frac{511}{900} = 0.568$ $\cos\theta = 1 - 0.568 = 0.432$ $\theta = \cos^{-1}(0.432) \approx 64.4°$

✅ Final Answer

$\theta \approx 64°$
💡 Energy-form Compton equation is useful when energies are given instead of wavelengths. Remember: $m_0 c^2 = 511$ keV for electrons.

📋 Given Information

Incident λ$\lambda = 2$ Å $= 2 \times 10^{-10}$ m

1 Find Scattered Wavelength

$\Delta\lambda = 0.02426(1 - \cos 60°) = 0.02426 \times 0.5 = 0.01213$ Å $\lambda' = 2 + 0.01213 = 2.01213$ Å

2 Energy of Recoil Electron

By conservation of energy: $E_{recoil} = E_{photon} - E'_{photon} = hc\left(\frac{1}{\lambda} - \frac{1}{\lambda'}\right)$

$E_{recoil} = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8 \left(\frac{1}{2 \times 10^{-10}} - \frac{1}{2.01213 \times 10^{-10}}\right)$ $= 1.988 \times 10^{-25} \times (5 \times 10^9 - 4.970 \times 10^9)$ $= 1.988 \times 10^{-25} \times 3.01 \times 10^7$ $= 5.98 \times 10^{-18}$ J $= 37.4$ eV

✅ Final Answer

$E_{recoil} \approx 37$ eV

PL-1Calculate the average energy of an oscillator of frequency ν = 5.6 × 10¹² Hz at T = 330 K, treating it as (a) a classical oscillator, and (b) a Planck oscillator.

📋 Given Information

Frequency$\nu = 5.6 \times 10^{12}$ Hz
Temperature$T = 330$ K

In classical physics, average energy per oscillator = $kT$ (equipartition theorem):

$\langle E \rangle_{classical} = kT = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 330$ $= 4.554 \times 10^{-21}$ J

2 Planck Average Energy

$\langle E \rangle_{Planck} = \dfrac{h\nu}{e^{h\nu/kT} - 1}$ $h\nu = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 5.6 \times 10^{12} = 3.71 \times 10^{-21}$ J $\dfrac{h\nu}{kT} = \dfrac{3.71 \times 10^{-21}}{4.554 \times 10^{-21}} = 0.815$ $\langle E \rangle = \dfrac{3.71 \times 10^{-21}}{e^{0.815} - 1} = \dfrac{3.71 \times 10^{-21}}{2.259 - 1} = \dfrac{3.71 \times 10^{-21}}{1.259}$ $= 2.95 \times 10^{-21}$ J

✅ Final Answer

Planck: $2.95 \times 10^{-21}$ J

📦 Particle in 1D Infinite Potential Well

★★ Most Important▶

The simplest quantum system: a particle trapped between two impenetrable walls at $x = 0$ and $x = L$. The potential is zero inside the box and infinite outside (the particle can NEVER escape).

What happens: The boundary conditions ($\psi = 0$ at walls) force the wave function to be standing waves — only certain wavelengths "fit." This directly leads to quantized energy levels.

Energy Eigenvalues
$$E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} = n^2 E_1 \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

Ground state ($n=1$): $E_1 = h^2/(8mL^2)$ — the zero-point energy. The particle is NEVER at rest!

Wave Functions
$$\psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \qquad 0 \leq x \leq L$$

Probability density: $|\psi_n|^2 = \frac{2}{L}\sin^2\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$

Key features:

  • $n = 1$: One half-wave fits → max probability at center ($x = L/2$)
  • $n = 2$: Two half-waves → max at $L/4$ and $3L/4$; ZERO probability at $L/2$ (node)
  • Spacing grows: $E_2 - E_1 = 3E_1$; $E_3 - E_2 = 5E_1$; $E_4 - E_3 = 7E_1$

🚇 Quantum Tunnelling

★ Theory + Numerical▶

Imagine throwing a ball at a hill that's taller than your throwing energy. Classical physics says: the ball bounces back, always. Quantum mechanics says: there's a non-zero probability the ball appears on the other side!

This is quantum tunnelling — the wave function doesn't drop to zero at the barrier; it decays exponentially through it. If the barrier is thin enough, there's a surviving wave on the other side.

Transmission Coefficient
$$T \approx e^{-2\kappa L} \qquad \kappa = \frac{\sqrt{2m(V_0 - E)}}{\hbar}$$

$L$ = barrier width, $V_0$ = barrier height, $E$ = particle energy ($E < V_0$)

$T$ increases when: barrier is thinner (smaller $L$), $E$ is closer to $V_0$, particle is lighter (smaller $m$)

Applications: Alpha decay, tunnel diodes, Josephson junctions, flash memory, and most importantly for exams — the Scanning Tunnelling Microscope (STM).

🔭 Scanning Tunnelling Microscope (STM)

4-5 marks▶

Principle: A sharp metallic tip is brought within ~1 nm of a conducting surface. Electrons tunnel across the vacuum gap → a measurable "tunnelling current" flows.

STM Current
$$I \propto e^{-2\kappa d}$$

A change of just 0.1 nm in gap $d$ changes current by a factor of ~10!

Working modes:

  • Constant current mode: Feedback loop adjusts tip height to maintain constant $I$ → height map = surface image
  • Constant height mode: Tip height fixed, $I$ varies → faster but risky for rough surfaces

Resolution: ~0.01 nm vertical, ~0.1 nm lateral — can image individual atoms!

Nobel Prize 1986: Binnig & Rohrer

Setup: Particle of mass $m$ in a box of length $L$. Potential $V = 0$ inside ($0 < x < L$), $V = \infty$ outside. Boundary conditions: $\psi(0) = 0$ and $\psi(L) = 0$.
Step 1Write TISE inside box

Inside the box, $V = 0$, so the TISE becomes:

$$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}\psi = 0$$

Define $k^2 = 2mE/\hbar^2$ to get: $\psi'' + k^2\psi = 0$

Step 2General solution
$$\psi(x) = A\sin(kx) + B\cos(kx)$$
Step 3Apply BC: $\psi(0) = 0$
$$\psi(0) = A\sin(0) + B\cos(0) = B = 0$$

Therefore: $\psi(x) = A\sin(kx)$

Step 4Apply BC: $\psi(L) = 0$
$$A\sin(kL) = 0$$

Since $A \neq 0$ (that would mean no particle): $\sin(kL) = 0$ → $kL = n\pi$ → $k = n\pi/L$

Step 5Energy eigenvalues

From $k^2 = 2mE/\hbar^2$ and $k = n\pi/L$:

$$E_n = \frac{k^2\hbar^2}{2m} = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2}$$

Since $\hbar = h/(2\pi)$: $\pi^2\hbar^2 = h^2/(4)$

✦ Energy Eigenvalues
$$\boxed{E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}} \qquad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$
Step 6Normalization

Find $A$ using: $\int_0^L |\psi|^2 dx = 1$

$$A^2 \int_0^L \sin^2\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx = A^2 \cdot \frac{L}{2} = 1 \implies A = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}$$
✦ Normalized Wave Function
$$\boxed{\psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)}$$

💡 LASER Physics

Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation

✨ Four Unique Properties of Laser Light

Short Theory▶

Laser light is fundamentally different from ordinary light (bulb, sun) in four key ways:

1. Monochromaticity: Laser emits an extremely pure single wavelength. Spectral linewidth $\Delta\nu$ can be as narrow as 100 Hz (vs $\sim 10^{14}$ Hz for sunlight).

2. Coherence — two types:

  • Spatial (transverse): All points across the beam front are in phase → beam maintains shape over long distances. Coherence length $L_c = \lambda^2/\Delta\lambda$.
  • Temporal (longitudinal): Phase relationship maintained over time → enables interference over long path differences.

3. Directionality: Laser beam divergence is typically milli-radians. A laser aimed at the Moon (384,000 km away) would only spread to ~5 km diameter. Ordinary light diverges in all directions.

4. High Intensity: All energy is concentrated in a narrow, coherent beam → extremely high power per unit area. A 1 mW He-Ne laser is ~10,000× brighter than sunlight per unit area.

🧮 Three Radiation Processes & Einstein Coefficients

★★ Most Important▶

Einstein (1917) identified three fundamental ways atoms interact with radiation between energy levels $E_1$ (lower) and $E_2$ (upper), where $E_2 - E_1 = h\nu$:

1. Stimulated Absorption — an atom in $E_1$ absorbs a photon of energy $h\nu$ and jumps to $E_2$:

Absorption Rate
$$R_{abs} = B_{12} \cdot \rho(\nu) \cdot N_1$$

2. Spontaneous Emission — an atom in $E_2$ drops to $E_1$ on its own, emitting a photon in a random direction with random phase. This is what ordinary light sources (bulbs) produce — incoherent.

Spontaneous Rate
$$R_{sp} = A_{21} \cdot N_2$$

3. Stimulated Emission — an incoming photon of energy $h\nu$ triggers an atom in $E_2$ to drop to $E_1$, emitting a photon that is identical in every way: same energy, phase, direction, polarization. This is the LASER mechanism!

Stimulated Emission Rate
$$R_{stim} = B_{21} \cdot \rho(\nu) \cdot N_2$$
✦ Einstein Relations
$$B_{12} = B_{21} \qquad \frac{A_{21}}{B_{21}} = \frac{8\pi h\nu^3}{c^3}$$

$A_{21}/B_{21} \propto \nu^3$ → spontaneous emission dominates at high frequencies → harder to make X-ray/UV lasers

🔄 Population Inversion & Pumping Schemes

★ Theory▶

The fundamental problem: At thermal equilibrium, $N_2/N_1 = e^{-h\nu/kT} \ll 1$. Almost all atoms are in the ground state. Net stimulated emission requires $N_2 > N_1$ — this non-equilibrium state is called population inversion.

2-level system: CANNOT achieve inversion. As you pump harder, the best you can do is $N_1 = N_2$ (absorption = stimulated emission) → saturation, no net amplification.

3-level system (e.g., Ruby laser):

  1. Pump atoms from $E_1$ (ground) to $E_3$ (pump band)
  2. Fast non-radiative decay: $E_3 \to E_2$ (metastable — long lifetime ~ms)
  3. Population builds up in $E_2$ → when $N_2 > N_1$ → laser emission $E_2 \to E_1$

Problem: Laser transition ends at ground state → need to pump >50% of all atoms. Typically pulsed operation only.

4-level system (e.g., He-Ne, Nd:YAG):

  1. Pump: $E_0 \to E_3$ (pump band)
  2. Fast decay: $E_3 \to E_2$ (metastable upper laser level)
  3. Laser: $E_2 \to E_1$ (lower laser level)
  4. Fast decay: $E_1 \to E_0$ — keeps $E_1$ nearly empty!

Result: Even small pumping gives inversion → low threshold → CW operation possible. Most practical lasers are 4-level.

📊 Threshold Gain Coefficient

Theory▶

Laser oscillation is sustained when gain ≥ losses per round trip.

Threshold Gain Coefficient
$$g_{th} = \alpha_{int} + \frac{1}{2L}\ln\!\left(\frac{1}{R_1 R_2}\right)$$

$\alpha_{int}$ = internal loss (scattering + absorption), $L$ = cavity length, $R_1, R_2$ = mirror reflectivities

Gain coefficient: $g = \sigma(N_2 - N_1)$ where $\sigma$ = stimulated emission cross section

Below threshold: spontaneous emission (LED-like). Above threshold: output increases sharply → coherent laser.

Detailed Laser Types

🔴 He-Ne Laser — The Classic Gas Laser

★ Most Asked▶

Wavelength: 632.8 nm (red) | Type: Gas, 4-level | Pumping: Electrical discharge | Mode: CW | Power: 0.5-50 mW

Construction:

  • Glass tube filled with He:Ne mixture in ratio 10:1 at low pressure (~1 torr)
  • Two electrodes for DC electrical discharge
  • Brewster angle windows at tube ends → minimize reflection losses, output is linearly polarized
  • External optical cavity: one mirror ~100% reflective, output coupler ~99% reflective

Working Mechanism (4 key steps — memorize this flow):

  1. Pumping: Electric discharge excites He atoms to metastable states (20.61 eV and 20.66 eV). He is pumped — NOT Ne!
  2. Resonant Energy Transfer: Excited He* collides with ground-state Ne → He returns to ground, Ne gets excited. This works because He metastable energies nearly match Ne upper levels. This is the key trick!
  3. Population Inversion: Ne atoms accumulate in upper laser levels (3s, 2s)
  4. Laser Emission: Ne transitions: 3s→2p gives 632.8 nm (red), also 3s→3p at 3.39 μm (IR), 2s→2p at 1.15 μm (IR)
  5. Lower levels depopulate via spontaneous emission → wall collisions → ground state

Why He:Ne = 10:1? More He atoms → more energy transfer to Ne → stronger inversion. Narrow tube → faster wall collisions for depleting lower Ne levels.

Applications: Holography, barcode scanners, laser printers, interferometry, optical alignment, educational labs.

🟢 Nd:YAG Laser — High-Power Solid State

★ Important▶

Nd:YAG = Neodymium ions (Nd³⁺) doped in Yttrium Aluminium Garnet (Y₃Al₅O₁₂). Nd³⁺ = lasing ion, YAG = transparent host crystal.

Wavelength: 1064 nm (near-IR) | Type: 4-level solid-state | Pumping: Optical (xenon flash lamp or laser diode) | Power: Up to 70+ W

Working:

  1. Flash lamp pumps Nd³⁺ from ground ⁴I₉/₂ → broad absorption bands (⁴F levels)
  2. Fast non-radiative decay to metastable ⁴F₃/₂ (lifetime ~230 μs)
  3. Laser transition: ⁴F₃/₂ → ⁴I₁₁/₂ emitting 1064 nm
  4. Fast decay ⁴I₁₁/₂ → ⁴I₉/₂ (ground) → lower level stays empty → efficient 4-level

Applications: Laser cutting/welding, medical surgery, LIDAR, range finding, frequency doubled to 532 nm (green laser pointer).

🔵 CO₂ Laser — Most Powerful CW

★ Important▶

Wavelength: 10.6 μm (far-IR) | Type: Molecular gas | Mix: CO₂:N₂:He | Power: Up to 10 kW! | Efficiency: ~30%

Key difference: Uses vibrational energy levels of CO₂ molecule (not electronic levels like other lasers). Three vibrational modes: symmetric stretch, bending, asymmetric stretch.

Step 2Solve for $\rho(\nu)$

Step 3Use Boltzmann

✦ Result 1

The probability of stimulated absorption equals stimulated emission

✦ Result 2
$$\boxed{\frac{A_{21}}{B_{21}} = \frac{8\pi h\nu^3}{c^3}}$$

MODULE 4 · FIBER OPTICS

🔆 Optical Fiber Communication

Guiding light through glass — total internal reflection, modes, losses

🏗 Fiber Structure & Total Internal Reflection

Foundation▶

An optical fiber is essentially a thin glass strand that traps light inside and carries it over long distances. Its structure:

  • Core: Central cylinder, refractive index $n_1$ (higher). Diameter: 5-100 μm depending on type
  • Cladding: Surrounds core, refractive index $n_2 < n_1$. Typically ~125 μm diameter
  • Protective jacket: Polymer coating for strength

Principle — Total Internal Reflection (TIR):

When light travels from a denser medium ($n_1$) to rarer medium ($n_2$), it bends away from the normal. If the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle $\theta_c$, the light is 100% reflected back — zero transmission.

Critical Angle
$$\sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta_c = \sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right)$$

Light entering the fiber core at the proper angle undergoes repeated TIR at the core-cladding boundary, zig-zagging down the fiber over kilometers.

📡 V-Parameter & Types of Fibers

Theory▶
V-Parameter (Normalized Frequency)
$$V = \frac{2\pi a}{\lambda} \cdot \text{NA} \qquad \text{Number of modes} \approx \frac{V^2}{2}$$

$V < 2.405$ → Single-mode  |  $V > 2.405$ → Multimode

Step-Index Single-Mode (SMF): Core 5-10 μm. Only fundamental mode propagates. No intermodal dispersion. Used for long-haul telecom.

Step-Index Multimode (MMF): Core 50-200 μm. Many modes → severe modal dispersion → limited bandwidth. Used for short links (LANs, medical).

Graded-Index Multimode (GRIN): Refractive index decreases parabolically from center: $n(r) = n_1[1 - 2\Delta(r/a)^2]^{1/2}$. Higher-order modes travel faster in lower-$n$ region → path time equalized → greatly reduced dispersion.

📉 Attenuation & Dispersion

★ Theory▶

Attenuation = signal power loss over distance.

Attenuation Formula
$$\alpha = \frac{10}{L}\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{P_{in}}{P_{out}}\right) \;\text{dB/km}$$

Causes: Rayleigh scattering ($\propto 1/\lambda^4$, dominant at short λ), OH⁻ absorption (water peaks at 1.38 μm), bending losses (macro and micro).

Minimum attenuation: ~0.2 dB/km at $\lambda = 1550$ nm — the telecom sweet spot.

Dispersion = pulse broadening (limits how fast you can send data).

Intermodal: Only in multimode fibers. Different modes travel different path lengths → arrive at different times. Eliminated in SMF.

Intramodal (chromatic): Even within one mode, different λ travel at different speeds.

  • Material dispersion: $n$ varies with $\lambda$ → different speeds
  • Waveguide dispersion: Mode shape changes with $\lambda$

At $\lambda = 1310$ nm, material and waveguide dispersion cancel → zero dispersion wavelength.

Acceptance Angle & NA — Full Derivation ⭐
Setup: Step-index fiber: core $n_1$, cladding $n_2$, surrounding medium $n_0 = 1$ (air). A ray enters the fiber at angle $\theta_i$ to the axis.
Step 1Snell's law at fiber face

At the air-core interface:

$$n_0 \sin\theta_i = n_1 \sin\theta_r \quad \Rightarrow \quad \sin\theta_i = n_1\sin\theta_r \quad \cdots(1)$$
Step 2Geometry inside core

The refracted ray hits the core-cladding interface at angle $\phi = 90° - \theta_r$ from the normal. For TIR: $\phi \geq \theta_c$.

$$\sin\theta_r = \cos\phi \quad \cdots(2)$$
Step 3Apply TIR condition

Maximum $\theta_i$ occurs when $\phi = \theta_c$ (just at TIR):

$$\sin\theta_{i,max} = n_1 \cos\theta_c = n_1\sqrt{1 - \sin^2\theta_c} = n_1\sqrt{1 - n_2^2/n_1^2}$$
✦ Numerical Aperture & Acceptance Angle
$$\boxed{\text{NA} = \sin\theta_a = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}}$$$$\boxed{\text{NA} = n_1\sqrt{2\Delta}} \qquad \Delta = \frac{n_1 - n_2}{n_1}$$

💎 Semiconductors & Optoelectronics

From electrons ↔ photons: LEDs, laser diodes, and photodetectors

🔴🔵 Direct vs Indirect Bandgap Semiconductors

★ Comparison Table▶

In a semiconductor, the relationship between energy ($E$) and crystal momentum ($k$) of electrons determines whether the material can efficiently emit light:

Direct bandgap: The conduction band minimum (CBM) and valence band maximum (VBM) occur at the same value of $k$. An electron can drop from CB to VB by emitting just a photon — no momentum change needed.

Indirect bandgap: CBM and VBM at different $k$ values. Transition requires both a photon (for energy) AND a phonon (lattice vibration, for momentum). This is a three-body process → very low probability → poor light emission.

💡 LED — Light Emitting Diode

★ Theory▶

Principle: A forward-biased p-n junction of a direct bandgap semiconductor. When current flows, electrons and holes are injected into the junction region where they recombine radiatively → emitting photons of energy $\approx E_g$. This is electroluminescence.

LED Wavelength
$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{E_g} = \frac{1240}{E_g\;(\text{eV})} \;\text{nm}$$

GaAs ($E_g = 1.42$ eV) → $\lambda = 873$ nm (IR)
GaN ($E_g = 3.4$ eV) → $\lambda = 365$ nm (UV/blue)
GaAsP → 630 nm (red)  |  InGaN → 450-520 nm (blue-green)

Key characteristics:

  • Spontaneous emission → light is incoherent with broad spectrum (~30-50 nm)
  • No threshold current — light output increases gradually with current
  • Good for displays, indicators, short-range fiber comm

  • Population inversion: Achieved at current above threshold ($I_{th}$) — heavily doped junction
  • Double heterostructure: Thin active layer (~0.1 μm GaAs) sandwiched between wider-bandgap AlGaAs cladding — confines both carriers and light

🔍 PN & PIN Photodiodes

★ Theory + Numerical▶

PN Photodiode: A reverse-biased p-n junction. Photons with $h\nu \geq E_g$ generate electron-hole pairs in the depletion region → swept by electric field → photocurrent.

Limitation: Thin depletion width → low absorption → poor efficiency.

PIN Photodiode (better!): Has a thick intrinsic (undoped) layer between P and N regions.

Advantages of PIN over PN:

  • Wide I-layer = large absorption volume → higher quantum efficiency
  • Lower junction capacitance ($C \propto 1/d$, d is wider) → faster response (>GHz)
  • Can operate at lower reverse bias
  • Lower dark current, lower noise
Quantum Efficiency
$$\eta = \frac{I_p/e}{P_{opt}/h\nu} = \frac{I_p \cdot h\nu}{e \cdot P_{opt}} = \frac{\text{photogenerated electrons/sec}}{\text{incident photons/sec}}$$
Responsivity
$$R = \frac{I_p}{P_{opt}} = \frac{\eta e}{h\nu} = \frac{\eta\lambda\;(\mu\text{m})}{1.24} \;\text{A/W}$$

PD-1A PIN photodiode at λ = 0.85 μm receives P = 10 μW of optical power and generates a photocurrent of Iₚ = 6.5 μA. Find the quantum efficiency and responsivity.

📋 Given Information

Wavelength$\lambda = 0.85$ μm
Optical power$P_{opt} = 10$ μW $= 10 \times 10^{-6}$ W
Photocurrent$I_p = 6.5$ μA $= 6.5 \times 10^{-6}$ A

1 Calculate Responsivity (the easy one first)

Responsivity is simply the ratio of photocurrent to optical power:

$R = \dfrac{I_p}{P_{opt}} = \dfrac{6.5 \times 10^{-6}}{10 \times 10^{-6}}$ $R = 0.65$ A/W
💡 This means for every 1 Watt of light hitting the detector, you get 0.65 Amperes of current. A perfect detector would give $R_{max} = e\lambda/(hc) \approx 0.685$ A/W at this wavelength.

2 Calculate Quantum Efficiency — Method 1 (from first principles)

Count photons per second and electrons per second separately:

Photons arriving per second: $E_{photon} = \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{0.85 \times 10^{-6}}$ $= 2.337 \times 10^{-19}$ J per photon $N_{photons} = \dfrac{P_{opt}}{E_{photon}} = \dfrac{10 \times 10^{-6}}{2.337 \times 10^{-19}} = 4.28 \times 10^{13}$ photons/s
Electron-hole pairs generated per second: $N_{e-h} = \dfrac{I_p}{e} = \dfrac{6.5 \times 10^{-6}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 4.06 \times 10^{13}$ e-h pairs/s
Quantum efficiency: $\eta = \dfrac{N_{e-h}}{N_{photons}} = \dfrac{4.06 \times 10^{13}}{4.28 \times 10^{13}} = 0.949 = 94.9\%$

3 Verify — Method 2 (quick formula)

$\eta = \dfrac{R \times 1.24}{\lambda\;(\mu\text{m})} = \dfrac{0.65 \times 1.24}{0.85}$ $= \dfrac{0.806}{0.85} = 0.949 = 94.9\%$ ✓
💡 Both methods give the same answer. Use Method 2 for speed in exams, but show Method 1 if the question asks you to "derive" or "show working."

✅ Final Answer

Responsivity: $R = 0.65$ A/W
Quantum Efficiency: $\eta = 94.9\%$
💡 94.9% quantum efficiency is excellent! Nearly every photon absorbed produces an electron-hole pair. Real PIN photodiodes at 850 nm (using Si) routinely achieve 80-95% QE. At 1550 nm, InGaAs detectors achieve ~90%.

PD-6A p-n photodiode has quantum efficiency η = 70% for photons of energy E = 1.5 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. Calculate (a) the wavelength, and (b) the optical power required to achieve a photocurrent of Iₚ = 3 μA.

QE$\eta = 0.70$

Photocurrent$I_p = 3$ μA $= 3 \times 10^{-6}$ A

PD-7A PIN photodiode generates one electron-hole pair per two incident photons at λ = 0.85 μm. Calculate (a) the quantum efficiency, (b) the maximum possible bandgap energy in eV, and (c) the mean photocurrent when incident optical power is P = 10 μW.

📋 Given Information

Condition1 electron per 2 photons → $\eta = 0.5$
Wavelength$\lambda = 0.85$ μm
Power$P = 10$ μW

1 Quantum Efficiency

$\eta = \dfrac{\text{electrons generated}}{\text{photons incident}} = \dfrac{1}{2} = 0.5 = 50\%$

2 Maximum Bandgap Energy

For the photodiode to respond at $\lambda = 0.85$ μm, the photon energy must exceed $E_g$. At the long-wavelength cutoff, $E_g = E_{photon}$:

$E_g \leq \dfrac{hc}{\lambda} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8}{0.85 \times 10^{-6}}$ $= \dfrac{1.988 \times 10^{-25}}{0.85 \times 10^{-6}}$ $= 2.338 \times 10^{-19}$ J $= \dfrac{2.338 \times 10^{-19}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 1.46$ eV

3 Mean Photocurrent

$R = \eta \cdot \dfrac{e\lambda}{hc} = 0.5 \times \dfrac{1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 0.85 \times 10^{-6}}{1.988 \times 10^{-25}}$ $= 0.5 \times 0.684 = 0.342$ A/W $I_p = R \times P = 0.342 \times 10 \times 10^{-6}$ $= 3.42 \times 10^{-6}$ A $= 3.42$ μA

✅ Final Answer

(a) $\eta = 50\%$
(b) $E_g \leq 1.46$ eV
(c) $I_p = 3.42$ μA
💡 Max bandgap = 1.46 eV at 0.85 μm. Si (1.12 eV) and GaAs (1.42 eV) both work at this wavelength! The "1 per 2 photons" is a clever way to say η = 50%.
